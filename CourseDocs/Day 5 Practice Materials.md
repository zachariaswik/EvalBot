# Day 5: Practice Tasks for Your Project

## Task 1. Find Your Investor - NOT READY FOR

INVESTORS

According to today’s lecture materials, finding the right investors is not about getting a
"yes" from anyone. It is about finding people who understand your space, believe in
your team, and want to help you grow.

Here is an example of a simple research pipeline:
1. Find similar companies - use Crunchbase, PitchBook, or CB Insights to find
startups similar to yours.
2. See who invested in them - look at their pre-seed and seed rounds; note which
investors led and which followed.
3. Check current activity - check their recent portfolio. Do they still invest in
similar deals?
4. Build your target list - create a list of 15-20 investors who are the best fit.

## Task 2. Elevator Pitch - NOT READY FOR INVESTORS

Although this task may seem very similar to the Conversation Pitch from the Day 4
materials, the main difference between them is the target audience - this time, you are
addressing investors instead of customers. Because of that, the focus and structure
should be different.

Recommended structure:
1. Problem - state the problem in concrete terms; make it personal and specific.
2. Solution and Proof - explain what you built and why it works; include one quick
proof point.
3. The Ask - state what you are raising and what it unlocks.

Elevator pitch building pipeline:
1. Write a long version - do not edit yet, put everything on paper, aim for 200-300
words.
2. Cut it in half - remove anything that is not essential, get down to 100-150
words.

| Future potential investors: Brighteye Ventures | VC firm focused on early- stage EdTech. | Attend their annual Brighteye Live event in London; their Sidekick community newsletter has a subscriber list of 13,000+ founders and operators — engage publicly and you'll meet portfolio founders who can intro you |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Emerge Education | Focused on learning space and future of work, specialist on early stage funding. | Connect with one on of their venture partners on LinkedIn, build a relationship, and ask for an internal intro to the partners |
| Sparkmind.vc | Nordic VC focused on learning sector. K-12, higher and secondary | Connect with founder in their portfolio on Linkedin. |
|  |  |  |

3. Cut it again - now be ruthless, remove every word that does not carry weight,
aim for 60-80 words.
4. Test it out loud - say it out loud from start to finish. Time yourself - it should
take between 45 and 60 seconds. Listen carefully. Does it sound clear? Or does
it feel awkward or forced?
5. Test it on real people - share it with five people using the "Is it just me?" test.
Refine based on what you hear.

## Task 3. Full Investor Pitch - NOT READY FOR

INVESTORS

This is the most complete version of your project presentation. However, keep in mind
that the full investor pitch should support your elevator pitch - not contradict it or add
new ideas. Keep your message consistent across all formats: elevator pitch, full pitch,
deck, and follow-up materials.

Recommended structure:
1. Hook and Problem - describe ONE clear and urgent problem. Make it personal
and concrete. Show the pain in time, money, or frustration. Use a real example
from your user interviews.
[For example: "Last year, we spoke with over 40 small e-commerce founders.
Almost all of them said they waste up to 15 hours a week copying order data
between Shopify, Google Sheets, and email - just to keep their teams aligned. It
slows down operations, causes mistakes, and makes it hard to grow."]

2. Solution and Live Demo - explain your unique approach. Focus on outcomes
you deliver, not just features. Show why NOW is the right time. Highlight your
unfair insight or advantage.
[For example: "We built a lightweight automation tool made just for e-commerce
teams. It pulls order and shipping data from your platforms and sends it to the
right Slack channels in real time. You can set it up in under five minutes with zero
code.

Unlike Zapier, we do not ask users to build automations themselves. We baked in
templates based on the 20 most common workflows we saw during research.
And it updates in real time, so your team always has the latest info without
asking."]

3. Traction Proof - show your early traction - website visits, sign‑ups, replies,
conversions, pilots, or testimonials. Small numbers are just fine if they show real
progress.
[For example: "In the last 6 weeks, we onboarded 12 teams. They are using our
product for order tracking, team updates, and delivery alerts. Three of them have
used it daily for over a month. One team says it saves them about five hours per
week. We also got our first $99/month subscription."]

4. Market and Competitors - now talk about the opportunity size. Show market
trends that support your timing. Explain your entry niche and how you can
expand later. Map the main players. Explain how you are different and better for
your niche. Remember: Saying “we have no competitors” is a huge red flag for
investors. Keep this simple. Use round numbers and easy sources. Avoid
complex charts. And most importantly - be honest!
[For example: "There are over 2 million small e-commerce teams globally. They
are drowning in tools that do not talk to each other. The market for e-commerce
operations software is over $30 billion and growing.
Our entry point is Shopify teams doing up to $500K per year. We do not
compete directly with Zapier or Airtable. We offer focused, fast workflows built
for non-technical founders."]

5. Business Model and Financials - explain how you make money. Make it simple:
who pays, how much they pay, how often they pay. Then mention your main
assumptions. For example: how many customers you expect to get, how fast
you think you can grow, and what your main costs will be. Use your 12-month
projection as a guide. You do not need perfect numbers - just show that your
thinking is clear and realistic.
[For example: "We charge $99 per month per workspace. Our 12-month goal is
to reach $15K MRR from 150 active teams. Costs are low — mostly cloud
hosting and support. We can break even at around 300 teams."]

6. Team - next, tell why you and your team can pull this off. Mention relevant
experience and skills. Share what you have already built together. Name the key
hires you plan to make. Investors tend to remember the faces and the energy
here.
[For example: "I am Alex, and I led ops for a Shopify brand that scaled to $2
million in two years. My co-founder, Dana, is a full-stack engineer who worked on

B2B integrations at Zapier. We built our tool ourselves, and we have worked
together on two shipping automation tools before."]

7. The Ask - and finally, state what you are raising. Explain what you will achieve
with this capital. Give a rough timeline for raising and closing.
At the end of your pitch, you can also mention any investors who are already
supporting you. This helps show that others already believe in your project. It
gives credibility and makes new investors more confident. It also shows that you
have a structured plan for raising and growing.
[For example: "We are raising $250,000 to onboard 200 teams, build more
templates, and test paid acquisition channels. We aim to close this round in 8
weeks.
So far, $50,000 is committed from one angel investor. We are also part of
AltaLab, which provides support and structure during this raise and beyond."]
