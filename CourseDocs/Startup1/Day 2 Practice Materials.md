## Task 1. SWOT Analysis

Below is the classical SWOT analysis table. What makes this one different from most
others is the approach to completing it: instead of nice clichés and a “dream story”,
use what you actually heard during the Day 1 interviews.

We encourage you to answer honestly: this is not the pitch deck you will use to
impress your investors; this is the analysis that will help you decide where to lean in
and where to protect your focus. If you skip this step now, you risk paying for it later.

Ask yourself the following questions to complete the corresponding sections:

Strengths:
• What did people say we are genuinely good at?
Does our team have real expertise in this space?
•
• Do we have access to users, data, or distribution that others don’t?
• Is our value proposition clearer than the typical vague “AI for everything” pitch?

Weaknesses:
• What was hard to explain during interviews?
• Which critical skills are missing on the team? Technical? Sales? Domain
knowledge?
• Are we trying to cover too many use cases at once?
• Are we relying too heavily on one person, one dataset, or one assumption?

Opportunities:
• Are there strong tailwinds - like new regulations, AI adoption, remote work
trends?
• Did you identify a segment where current tools just don’t cut it?
• Did someone mention a related problem they’d love to solve next?

Threats:

• Is a big platform gearing up to enter your space?
• Are there trust, privacy, or security concerns you must handle?
• Are your users locked into old workflows or long-term contracts?

## Task 3. One-Pager

Use the next page to create the shortest, sharpest version of your startup story. Below
we provide a structure you can follow (however, you don’t have to stick to it word for
word; feel free to adjust it according to your project):

1.Problem and Context

Students are constantly exposed to distractions and harmful content with unrestricted
browsing when they want to focus on their studies. The current parental-controls are
either too fragile or too restrictive.

2. Solution and Value Proposition

We provide a kiosk-mode learning environment with a browser and workspace that
shelter the student from distractions and harmful content, so they can stay focused and
engaged.

3. Target Customer

| Cost Structure The running costs are small. We need a webpage (little to no startup costs) + database (little to no startup costs). Currently, there’s no LLM’s involved, so there’s little to no API costs. | Revenue Streams I will get revenue from subscriptions. B2C (specifically to teachers/parents), and B2B (school wide). |
| --- | --- |

The target users are K-12 students, that use computers for their studies. The target
customer are their parents or teachers.

4. Evidence

Teachers telling me they wanted to support the idea and to be early testers together
with their students. Parents saying they would be interested in the tool. Decision
makers in the education field seeing how this application could help with various
problems in Education, and offering help for me to reach out through their networks.

5. SWOT Highlights

Strengths + Opportunities:

Agentic coding - will significantly speed up development.

There’s a high need, especially with unrestricted AI chat’s in schools, for a tool that
make that cheating option less feasible.

There’s an overall big push toward making technology more suitable for students (A
recent big topic and worry has been circulating around the term “brain-rot”).

Weaknesses + Threats

Lack of resources. (1-man team + [privacy concerns + potentially tricky sales)].

6. Business Model Snapshot

I plan to reach my customers through the channel educator/teacher/school networks.

Early stage-costs are minimal, and will scale linearly as my customer base increase. The
initial launch will focus on B2C, so that we can get early adopters and testers. The next
phase will be to focus on B2B, where we sell school-wide licenses.

Market size: There are roughly than 100 million devices used by K-12 students in
Europe + USA.
