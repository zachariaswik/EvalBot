# Agent 4 — Product & Positioning Analyst: Prompts

## 1. Killer Feature

**1.1** The core product is "a browser with a Study Mode button → kiosk mode → locked tabs → filtered search." Is this a killer feature that drives adoption on its own, or a utility feature that's useful but unremarkable? What would make a parent or student say "I NEED this" rather than "that's nice"?

**1.2** The founder's evidence includes "teachers wanting to be early testers" and "parents saying they would be interested." On a scale of "polite interest" to "shut up and take my money," where does this evidence sit? What signals would indicate a genuine killer feature vs. a nice-to-have?

**1.3** The founder identifies "AI being used by students for cheating" as an opportunity. Could AI access management — intelligently allowing educational AI (Khan Academy) while blocking unrestricted AI chat (ChatGPT, open Gemini) — become the killer feature instead of general distraction blocking? Would that be sharper and more urgent?

## 2. Wedge Market

**2.1** The founder targets "K-12 students who use computers for studies, at home and/or in school" — an extremely broad segment. What is the sharpest possible wedge market? Consider: age group, device type, use context (home vs. school), buyer type (parent vs. teacher vs. student), and geography. Where does the product solve the most acute pain?

**2.2** The plan is B2C first (parents/teachers) then B2B (school-wide licenses). Is this the right sequencing? Given the founder's constraints (solo, full-time job, minimal budget) and existing educator network, would a specific B2B niche (single school district, tutoring center, homeschool co-op, exam prep program) be a better wedge entry point?

**2.3** The product is described as a "desktop" application. Most US K-12 students use Chromebooks (60%+ share); Europe is mixed Windows/Chromebook/iPad. Does the desktop-first approach define the wedge or limit it? What platform should be targeted first for maximum wedge penetration?

## 3. Moat Potential

**3.1** What defensible moat could this product build over time? Evaluate each type: (a) Network effects, (b) Data moat from usage patterns, (c) Switching costs for families/schools, (d) Brand/trust in student digital wellness, (e) Technical barriers to replication, (f) Ecosystem/integration lock-in with LMS or school tools. Which moat type is most realistic for a solo founder at pre-seed?

**3.2** Could community become the moat? If the product builds a community of parents and educators who co-create content policies (whitelists/blacklists), share what works, and advocate for the product, could that community create defensibility that Big Tech platforms can't replicate? Evaluate a community-driven moat hypothesis.

**3.3** The founder claims the product is "not competing against previous workflows — and can therefore be adopted with less friction." If adoption friction is low, are switching costs also low? What would create lock-in over time (session history, learning analytics, compliance reporting, LMS integration)?

## 4. Positioning

**4.1** The current positioning is: "A kiosk-mode learning environment with a browser and workspace that shelters the student from distractions and harmful content." Is this clear, differentiated, and buyer-centric? Rewrite using: "For [target], who [need], [product] is the [category] that [key benefit], unlike [alternatives], because [reason to believe]."

**4.2** Evaluate four positioning options: (a) better-designed parental control tool, (b) student productivity tool with protections, (c) school compliance tool for device-use policies, (d) "digital study room" — a new category. For each: who is the buyer, what is the competitive set, what is the pricing ceiling, and how defensible is the position?

**4.3** The Day 4 campaign targets students directly ("If you're having problems staying focused...") but the buyer is the parent/teacher. Should marketing target the user (student) or the buyer (parent)? Can it do both? What does messaging look like for each audience?

## 5. AI Wrapper Assessment

**5.1** The product currently uses no AI ("no LLMs involved, little to no API costs") and the founder explicitly says "the selling-point is not AI." Is this a strength (not an AI wrapper, genuine problem-solving) or a weakness (competitors use AI for smarter filtering, behavioral analysis, adaptive restrictions)? Should the founder lean into simplicity or plan to add AI capabilities?

**5.2** If AI were added, rank these by value-add and solo-founder feasibility: (a) AI-powered content categorization (auto-classify sites as educational vs. distracting), (b) AI study session recommendations based on behavior, (c) AI-generated study summaries from session content, (d) AI-based "difficulty adjustment" that loosens restrictions as students demonstrate focus. Which adds the most product value without becoming an AI wrapper?

## 6. Product Risks

**6.1** Identify and rank the top 5 product risks by severity and likelihood: (a) students bypassing kiosk mode, (b) product being too restrictive (students resent it), (c) content filtering errors (blocking educational sites or missing harmful content), (d) cross-platform compatibility, (e) COPPA/GDPR-K privacy compliance, (f) false sense of security for parents, (g) browser performance issues.

**6.2** The execution roadmap targets 5 alpha users (March 16) → 10 beta users (April 20) → 50+ users (June 15). Is this timeline appropriately paced given the market timing window (41 states with phone bans, COPPA deadline April 2026, cultural moment)? What are the risks of moving too slowly vs. too fast?

## 7. Focus Recommendation

**7.1** Provide a single focus recommendation: Should the founder (a) race to 100+ users with current product, (b) pivot the killer feature toward AI access management for students, (c) narrow the wedge to one specific niche and go deep, (d) build community/trust moat first before scaling, or (e) something else? Justify with evidence from the full analysis.
