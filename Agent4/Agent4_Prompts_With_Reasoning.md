# Agent 4 — Product & Positioning Analyst: Prompts with Reasoning

## Overview

These prompts are designed to determine whether the product has the potential to become large — not just viable, but venture-scale. Agent 4 looks past the market (Agent 3's territory) and into the product itself: is there a killer feature, a defensible wedge, a real moat, or is this just another tool that works until someone bigger copies it? Every prompt is grounded in what the founder has already written in the practice materials.

---

## Section 1: Killer Feature Identification

### Prompt 1.1

> The founder describes the core product as "a browser with a Study Mode button, which lets the user quickly create a focus session with the tabs conducive to learning. When the user enters this focus session, a kiosk mode opens up, where URL bar is disabled and web search is highly filtered." Is this a killer feature — one that is so compelling it drives adoption on its own — or is it a utility feature that's useful but not remarkable? What would make a student or parent say "I NEED this" rather than "that's nice"?

**Reasoning:** The Day 3 sales pitch describes the product mechanics clearly: Study Mode button → kiosk mode → locked tabs → filtered search. This is functional but potentially unremarkable — it's what a parent could approximate with Screen Time settings in 5 minutes. A killer feature needs to be 10x better than the alternative, not just marginally different. The founder needs honest feedback on whether the core mechanic generates urgency or just mild interest. The Day 2 evidence section mentions "parents saying they would be interested" — interest is weaker than desperation.

### Prompt 1.2

> Review the founder's user interview evidence: "Teachers telling me they wanted to support the idea and to be early testers together with their students. Parents saying they would be interested in the tool. Decision makers in the education field seeing how this application could help with various problems in Education." On a scale of "polite interest" to "shut up and take my money," where does this evidence sit? What signal would indicate a genuine killer feature vs. a nice-to-have?

**Reasoning:** The Day 2 One-Pager lists this evidence as proof of demand. But "interested" and "could help" are weak signals — people say these things to be polite. The Day 2 instructions explicitly warn against "nice clichés and a dream story." Agent 4 needs to assess whether the founder has evidence of real pull (people asking when they can use it, offering to pay, following up unprompted) or merely polite encouragement. The Day 3 metrics (90-minute focus sessions, user ratings) haven't been tested yet — they're targets, not data.

### Prompt 1.3

> The founder mentions the product handles "AI being used by students for cheating rather than for learning" as an opportunity. Could AI restriction/management become the killer feature rather than distraction blocking? If the product could intelligently allow educational AI (Khan Academy, approved tools) while blocking unrestricted AI chat (ChatGPT, open Gemini), would that be a sharper, more urgent value proposition than general distraction blocking?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Opportunities section identifies AI cheating as a market opportunity, and the cultural moment around AI in schools is intense (school districts banning ChatGPT, then unbanning it, then struggling with policy). If the product pivots its killer feature from "blocks TikTok" (solvable with Screen Time) to "manages AI access for students" (much harder for parents to configure manually), the urgency and differentiation both increase dramatically. This is a reframing question that could change the product's entire trajectory.

## Section 2: Wedge Market Identification

### Prompt 2.1

> The founder's Day 4 materials identify the growth channel as social media (Reddit, TikTok, Discord study communities) targeting "students who find it hard to stay focused." The Business Model Canvas says the target is "K-12 students who use computers for studies. At home and, or in school." This is extremely broad. What is the sharpest possible wedge market — the single most specific segment where this product solves the most acute pain? Consider age group, device type, use context (home vs. school), buyer (parent vs. teacher vs. student), and geography.

**Reasoning:** "K-12 students" spans ages 5-18, which is an absurdly wide range. A 6-year-old on an iPad has fundamentally different needs than a 16-year-old on a Windows laptop. The SWOT notes the founder is "covering too much at the same time" and has "several use cases" without knowing which to focus on. Agent 4's job is to find the wedge — the beachhead where the product wins decisively before expanding. The Day 4 channel work (Reddit, TikTok, Discord) implicitly suggests older students (13+) who self-organize in study communities, which contradicts the B2C "parent buys for child" model. This tension needs resolving.

### Prompt 2.2

> The founder plans to start B2C (parents/teachers) then move to B2B (school-wide licenses). Evaluate this sequencing. Is B2C the right wedge, or should the founder consider starting with a specific B2B niche (e.g., a single school district, a tutoring center, a homeschool co-op, an exam prep program)? What are the pros and cons of each entry path given the founder's constraints (solo founder, full-time job, minimal budget)?

**Reasoning:** The Business Model Canvas and One-Pager both describe a B2C → B2B progression. But the Day 2 SWOT reveals the founder is a solo operator with a full-time job — selling B2C to individual parents one-by-one through social media is extraordinarily labor-intensive per dollar of revenue. Meanwhile, the founder has "contact with teacher/educator networks" and "decision makers in the education field offering help to reach out through their networks" — which sounds more like a B2B warm-intro pipeline than a B2C social media play. The wedge question is also a go-to-market question: where can a resource-constrained founder get the most traction with the least effort?

### Prompt 2.3

> The founder mentions their product works as a "kiosk mode application removing harmful and distracting and addictive content from desktop." Does the desktop-first approach define the wedge, or limit it? Most K-12 students in the US use Chromebooks (school-issued) or iPads. In Europe, the device mix is more varied. Should the founder's wedge market be defined by what platform the product currently runs on? What platform should it target first for maximum wedge penetration?

**Reasoning:** The Business Model Canvas describes a "desktop" application, but the K-12 device landscape is dominated by Chromebooks in the US (60%+ market share) and a mix of Windows/Chromebook/iPad in Europe. If the product only runs on Windows or Mac desktops, the wedge market is immediately constrained to home-use scenarios where parents have bought personal computers — which skews toward higher-income families. If it runs on Chromebooks, it can access the school market. Platform choice IS wedge choice. The founder hasn't addressed this in the materials.

## Section 3: Moat Potential

### Prompt 3.1

> What defensible moat could a kiosk-mode focus browser build over time? Evaluate each potential moat type: (a) Network effects — does the product get better as more people use it? (b) Data moat — does usage generate proprietary data that improves the product? (c) Switching costs — once a family or school adopts it, how painful is it to leave? (d) Brand/trust moat — can the product become the trusted name in student digital wellness? (e) Technical moat — is there any technology that's hard to replicate? (f) Ecosystem/integration moat — can it become embedded in a workflow that's hard to unplug?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Strengths list "agentic coding" as an advantage — but that's a development speed advantage, not a moat. Any competitor or platform can build the same features. The Day 5 materials mention potential investors like Brighteye Ventures and Emerge Education, who will absolutely ask "what's your moat?" A pre-seed startup doesn't need a moat yet, but it needs a credible moat hypothesis — a story for how defensibility emerges over time. Without one, the product is a feature waiting to be absorbed by a platform.

### Prompt 3.2

> The founder's SWOT intersection (S × O) highlights "Genuine interest + Current tools are insufficient." Could the moat come from community rather than technology? If the product builds a community of parents and educators who trust the brand, co-create content policies (whitelists/blacklists), and advocate for the product, could that community become the moat? Evaluate whether a community-driven approach (open-source content policies, parent forums, educator advisory board) could create defensibility that a Big Tech platform can't easily replicate.

**Reasoning:** The Day 4 channel strategy already targets communities (Reddit, Discord study groups, educator networks). If the product builds its own community around student digital wellness — where parents share what works, educators contribute to curated content policies, and students give feedback on the experience — that community becomes a moat. GoGuardian and Securly don't have parent communities; they sell to IT admins. Apple and Google don't offer community-curated content policies. This is a realistic moat hypothesis for a solo founder who can't compete on engineering resources.

### Prompt 3.3

> Assess the founder's claim that the application is "not competing against previous workflows — and can therefore be adopted with less friction." If adoption friction is low, does that also mean switching costs are low? In other words, if it's easy to start using, is it also easy to stop using? What would create lock-in over time?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Threats section makes a nuanced argument: because the product doesn't replace an existing workflow, it can be adopted alongside whatever the user already does. But this is a double-edged sword — if there's no workflow replacement, there's no switching cost. The user can drop it tomorrow with zero consequence. Agent 4 needs to identify what creates stickiness: session history, learning analytics for parents, school-wide compliance reporting, integration with LMS platforms, or something else entirely.

## Section 4: Positioning Recommendation

### Prompt 4.1

> The founder currently positions the product as: "A kiosk-mode learning environment with a browser and workspace that shelters the student from distractions and harmful content, so they can stay focused and engaged." Evaluate this positioning statement. Is it clear enough? Is it differentiated enough? Does it communicate the value in terms the buyer (parent or teacher) cares about? Rewrite the positioning statement in the format: "For [target], who [need], [product] is the [category] that [key benefit], unlike [alternatives], because [reason to believe]."

**Reasoning:** The Day 2 One-Pager has the current positioning. It's functional but generic — "shelters the student from distractions" could describe any parental control app. The Day 4 campaign message is slightly sharper: "If you're having problems staying focused on your work or studies, we've built a browser specially designed to help you maintain focus." But this speaks to the student, while the buyer is the parent. Positioning needs to match the buyer's language and pain points. A parent doesn't care about "kiosk mode" — they care about "my kid actually studied for 90 minutes without touching TikTok."

### Prompt 4.2

> Should the product be positioned as (a) a parental control tool that's better designed, (b) a student productivity tool that includes protections, (c) a school compliance tool that satisfies device-use policies, or (d) a "digital study room" that's a new category entirely? For each positioning option, assess: who the buyer is, what the competitive set becomes, what the pricing ceiling is, and how defensible the position is.

**Reasoning:** Positioning choice determines everything downstream: who you sell to, who you compete with, what you charge, and what investors compare you to. The founder's materials oscillate between all four of these framings. The SWOT uses "parental control" language. The One-Pager uses "learning environment" language. The Sales Pitch uses "browser with focus mode." The Day 4 channel targets student communities directly. Each framing puts the product in a different competitive context with different pricing expectations. Agent 4 needs to pick one and commit.

### Prompt 4.3

> The founder's Day 4 campaign message targets students directly: "If you're having problems staying focused on your work or studies..." But the buyer is the parent or teacher. Should the product be marketed to the user (student) or the buyer (parent/teacher)? Can it do both? What does the messaging need to look like for each audience, and which audience should be primary?

**Reasoning:** Day 4 reveals a classic two-sided positioning challenge: the user and the buyer are different people. Students don't want to be locked down (they'll resist). Parents want their kids to focus (they'll pay). The founder's social media strategy targets study communities where students hang out — but students discovering a "blocking browser" in a study community might react negatively ("my parents would love this, I'd hate it"). Alternatively, if positioned as a tool students voluntarily use to help themselves focus (like Forest), the adoption dynamic changes entirely. The messaging strategy needs to resolve this tension.

## Section 5: AI Wrapper Assessment

### Prompt 5.1

> The founder states: "Currently, there's no LLMs involved, so there's little to no API costs." The SWOT Strengths mention "the direct selling-point is not AI, but to deal with a real problem." Evaluate: is this product at risk of being perceived as "just an AI wrapper" despite not currently using AI? Conversely, does the absence of AI in the product create a risk that it falls behind competitors who use AI for smarter content filtering, behavioral analysis, or adaptive restriction levels?

**Reasoning:** The founder explicitly distances the product from "AI for everything" positioning — which is smart at a time when investors are skeptical of AI wrappers. But the competitive landscape tells a different story: GoGuardian uses AI for behavioral analysis, Securly uses AI for sentiment detection, Bark uses AI for content monitoring. If the product doesn't use AI at all, it may be perceived as less sophisticated. The question is whether the founder should (a) lean into the "no AI needed" simplicity as a feature, or (b) plan to add AI capabilities (smart filtering, adaptive session length, learning analytics) as differentiators.

### Prompt 5.2

> If the founder were to add AI capabilities, which of these would create the most product value: (a) AI-powered content categorization that automatically determines whether a website is educational vs. distracting (reducing manual whitelist maintenance), (b) AI-driven study session recommendations based on the student's behavior patterns, (c) AI-generated study summaries or notes from the content the student accessed during a session, or (d) AI-based "difficulty adjustment" that gradually loosens restrictions as the student demonstrates focus consistency? Rank these by value-add and feasibility for a solo founder.

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Opportunities identify "AI being used by students for cheating" as a problem to solve, but the product itself doesn't use AI. If the founder adds AI, it should be purposeful — not "AI because investors want AI" but "AI because it solves a real product problem." The content categorization option (a) addresses the biggest operational challenge of any filtering product: keeping the whitelist/blacklist current. The behavior-adaptive option (d) addresses the UX problem the founder identifies: tools that are "too restrictive" make students resentful. These are genuine product improvements, not wrapper features.

## Section 6: Product Risks

### Prompt 6.1

> Identify the top 5 product risks — things that could go wrong with the product itself (not the market or the team). Consider: (a) Students finding workarounds to escape kiosk mode, (b) The product being too restrictive and students hating it, (c) Content filtering being too aggressive (blocking legitimate educational sites) or too permissive (missing harmful content), (d) Cross-platform compatibility challenges, (e) Privacy/data collection concerns under COPPA/GDPR-K, (f) The product creating a false sense of security for parents, (g) Performance issues making the browser unusable for actual studying. Rank these by severity and likelihood.

**Reasoning:** The SWOT identifies privacy as a threat and resource constraints as a weakness, but doesn't enumerate product-level risks. The Day 3 metrics (90-minute sustained focus, user experience rating of 5/5) are aspirational targets — what happens when reality falls short? Students are incredibly resourceful at bypassing restrictions (this is well-documented with school Chromebook bypasses). Content filtering is an endless cat-and-mouse game. These risks directly affect whether the product can deliver on its core promise.

### Prompt 6.2

> The founder's execution roadmap (Day 3) plans: alpha to 5 friends/family (March 16), then beta to 10 users through network (April 20), then wider launch at 50+ users (June 15). Assess whether this timeline is aggressive enough, too slow, or appropriately paced given the competitive landscape and market timing window. What are the risks of moving too slowly vs. too fast?

**Reasoning:** The regulatory and cultural timing window (41 states with phone bans, COPPA 2026 deadline in April, "brain rot" cultural peak) suggests urgency. But the founder has a full-time job and is working part-time (40h over 2 weeks for milestone 2). The execution roadmap reaches 50 users by mid-June 2026, which is 3 months from now. In that time, Google could ship a Chromebook study mode, Apple could enhance Screen Time, or a well-funded startup could launch a competing product. Agent 4 needs to assess whether the pace matches the opportunity window.

## Section 7: Focus Recommendation

### Prompt 7.1

> Based on all the analysis above, provide a single focus recommendation for the founder. Should the founder: (a) Double down on the current product as-is and race to get 100+ users, (b) Pivot the killer feature toward AI access management for students (allowing educational AI, blocking unrestricted AI), (c) Narrow the wedge to a specific niche (e.g., homeschool families, exam prep centers, a single school district) and go deep, (d) Build the community/trust moat first (parent forums, educator advisory board, open content policies) before scaling the product, or (e) Something else entirely? Justify the recommendation with evidence from the analysis.

**Reasoning:** This is Agent 4's capstone output — the "focus recommendation." The founder's own materials reveal a pattern of breadth over depth: multiple use cases, broad target market (K-12), multiple buyer segments (B2C and B2B), multiple channels. The Day 2 SWOT explicitly flags "covering too much at the same time" as a weakness. Agent 4's job is to cut through the optionality and recommend one path. The recommendation must balance urgency (market window is open now) with the founder's constraints (solo, part-time, no funding).
