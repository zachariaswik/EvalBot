# Agent 5 — Founder Fit Analyst: Prompts with Reasoning

## Overview

Agent 5 evaluates the founder, not the product or market. The question is: even if the market is real and the product concept is sound, is THIS founder the right person to build THIS company? The prompts are designed to extract honest signal from what the founder has written (and not written) across the practice materials. Unlike Agents 3 and 4, this analysis is almost entirely inward-facing — the course materials themselves are the primary evidence.

## Section 1: Founder-Market Fit

### Prompt 1.1

> The founder is a software developer building a product for K-12 students, with parents and teachers as the buyer. Evaluate founder-market fit: Does the founder have a personal connection to the K-12 education space — as a parent, former teacher, school administrator, or education researcher? Or is this a technically-driven founder entering a market they find interesting but don't deeply inhabit? What does the evidence in the practice materials reveal about the founder's proximity to the problem?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT mentions "contact with teacher/educator networks (both in the US and Europe)" and "decision makers in the education field offering help." But the materials never say the founder IS a teacher, parent of a K-12 student, or education professional. The Business Model Canvas says "I depend on my network with Educators" — which implies the founder is adjacent to the market, not inside it. Founder-market fit is strongest when the founder lives the problem daily. A developer who builds a focus browser because they think it's a good idea is weaker than a frustrated parent who happens to know how to code.

### Prompt 1.2

> The founder identifies "shortened attention span," "brain-rot," and "AI cheating" as the driving problems. These are societal concerns that affect millions of families. Assess whether the founder's understanding of these problems comes from first-hand experience (their own children, their own classroom, their own school) or from observation and general awareness. Does the founder demonstrate deep empathy for the end user (the student) and the buyer (the parent/teacher), or is the understanding more intellectual than experiential?

**Reasoning:** The Day 3 sales pitch opens with: "Over the last decade, there's been a significant increase in distracting and addictive applications in technology..." This reads like a general observation, not a personal pain story. Compare it to the Day 5 investor pitch example: "Last year, we spoke with over 40 small e-commerce founders. Almost all of them said they waste up to 15 hours a week..." — that's specific, experiential evidence. The founder's pitch lacks this specificity. An investor will notice. If the founder doesn't have a personal "why" — their own child struggling, their own classroom falling apart — they need to develop one through deep user immersion.

### Prompt 1.3

> The founder mentions having "a network where I can get specialist input from the fields of computer science, design, marketing, education, entrepreneurship and business." Evaluate the quality of this network claim. Is this a deep, activated network (people who have committed time, resources, or expertise) or a broad, passive network (people the founder knows but hasn't mobilized)? What evidence exists in the materials that the network is actually being used?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Strengths list this network as an advantage, but the evidence is thin. The Day 2 materials mention "contact with teacher/educator networks" and "decision makers offering help to reach out through their networks." The Day 5 investor research identifies three potential investors (Brighteye Ventures, Emerge Education, Sparkmind.vc) with specific outreach strategies. But the founder also says "NOT READY FOR INVESTORS" three times in Day 5. A strong network should accelerate readiness, not delay it. If the network is passive ("people I could call") rather than active ("people who are helping me now"), it's a weaker asset than claimed.

## Section 2: Domain Expertise

### Prompt 2.1

> What domain expertise does the founder demonstrate in K-12 education, child development, digital wellness, or child safety? Review the practice materials for evidence of domain knowledge: understanding of school procurement cycles, familiarity with COPPA/FERPA/GDPR-K requirements, knowledge of how schools deploy and manage devices, awareness of student psychology around restriction and autonomy. What is present and what is missing?

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Threats mention "privacy concern that needs to be handled correctly" — but there's no evidence the founder understands COPPA requirements (verifiable parental consent, data minimization, the April 2026 deadline). The Business Model Canvas says the product targets "K-12 students who use computers for studies" — a description that could be written by anyone, no domain knowledge required. The One-Pager mentions "kiosk-mode learning environment" — a technical concept, not an educational one. Agent 5 needs to flag whether the founder has the domain depth to navigate the regulatory, institutional, and psychological complexities of the K-12 space, or whether they'll need to acquire this expertise quickly.

### Prompt 2.2

> The K-12 EdTech space has specific challenges that differ from consumer software: long school procurement cycles (6-18 months), pilot-to-purchase conversion friction, IT department gatekeeping, FERPA/COPPA compliance requirements, district-level budget approvals, and teacher change management. Does the founder show awareness of any of these challenges in the practice materials? If not, what is the risk of entering K-12 without this knowledge?

**Reasoning:** The Business Model Canvas mentions B2B school-wide licenses as a revenue stream, and the SWOT acknowledges "education institutions are tight on budget." But there's no mention of procurement cycles, IT department approvals, pilot programs, or compliance certifications. The Day 5 investor research identifies EdTech-focused VCs (Brighteye, Emerge, Sparkmind) — which shows awareness of the ecosystem — but no evidence of understanding the operational reality of selling to schools. This is a common failure mode for technical founders entering EdTech: they build a great product and then discover that selling to schools takes 12-18 months of relationship-building, pilots, and compliance documentation.

## Section 3: Execution Ability

### Prompt 3.1

> The founder acknowledges being "a 1-man team" with "a full-time job" and says "time is a big limitation." The execution roadmap (Day 3) allocates 40 hours over 2 weeks for Milestone 2 and 80 hours over 1 month for Milestone 4. Assess the founder's execution capacity realistically. Can a solo founder with a full-time job build, launch, and scale a product in the K-12 space? What historical analogies exist — successful solo founders who built companies while employed full-time? What is the realistic output per week, and does the roadmap match it?

**Reasoning:** The Day 3 roadmap is the most concrete execution evidence. 40 hours over 2 weeks = 20 hours/week, which is aggressive for someone with a full-time job. 80 hours over 1 month = 20 hours/week sustained for a month. This is possible but unsustainable for more than a few months — especially once customer support, marketing, community management, and compliance work are added to pure development. The SWOT Weakness explicitly identifies this: "Currently I'm a 1-man team, and I already have a full-time job. It is hard to find enough time. Time is a big limitation." Agent 5 needs to assess whether the execution plan is realistic or aspirational.

### Prompt 3.2

> The founder mentions "agentic coding" as a key strength multiple times — in the SWOT Strengths and in the S×O intersection ("Agentic Coding/Fast development + Changing school needs"). Evaluate how much of an execution multiplier this actually is. Can agentic coding (AI-assisted development) meaningfully compensate for being a solo founder with limited time? Where does it help (building features, writing code) and where doesn't it help (sales, compliance, design, user research, community building)?

**Reasoning:** "Agentic coding" appears 3 times across the materials — it's clearly something the founder considers a major advantage. And it is real: AI-assisted development can 2-5x a developer's output on certain tasks (generating boilerplate, writing tests, building UI components). But the challenges this startup faces are not primarily coding challenges. The hard problems are: understanding the market (user research), navigating compliance (COPPA, GDPR-K), selling to schools (relationship building), building trust with parents (brand), and creating a community (engagement). None of these can be accelerated by agentic coding. If the founder is over-indexing on coding speed as the key advantage, they may be under-investing in the non-technical work that actually determines success.

### Prompt 3.3

> Review the founder's execution milestones (Day 3) and marketing campaign plan (Day 4). Are these plans specific enough to execute, or are they aspirational outlines? For each milestone, assess: Is the success criterion measurable and specific? Is the resource allocation realistic? Is the deadline achievable? Is there a clear next step if the milestone fails?

**Reasoning:** The Day 3 milestones are structured but vague in places. Milestone 1 success criteria: "Feedback through conversations: 'Is the idea any good?' Push for honest feedback." This is qualitative and subjective — how does the founder know if the feedback is positive enough to continue? Milestone 3 success criteria: "Feedback through conversations: 'Can you see yourself using this application?'" Again qualitative. The Day 4 campaign plan says "100+ users within the first two weeks" for the waitlist — this is the only quantitative, falsifiable metric in all the materials. Agent 5 should assess whether the founder thinks in clear, measurable terms or defaults to vague qualitative benchmarks.

## Section 4: Technical Depth

### Prompt 4.1

> Based on the product description, assess the technical complexity of what the founder needs to build. A kiosk-mode browser that locks down the desktop, filters web content, disables the URL bar, and controls which sites are accessible involves: OS-level process management, browser engine integration or development, content filtering at the network/DNS/proxy level, cross-platform compatibility (Windows + Mac at minimum), kiosk security (preventing bypass via Task Manager, Alt+Tab, or process killing), and potentially a browser extension or standalone application. Is this achievable for a single developer? What are the hardest technical challenges?

**Reasoning:** The product description sounds simple ("a browser with a Study Mode button") but the technical implementation is non-trivial. Building a kiosk mode that students can't bypass requires OS-level hooks that differ dramatically between Windows and Mac. Content filtering needs to work across HTTPS (requiring SSL inspection or DNS-level filtering). The "kiosk security" problem is the hardest: students will try to kill the process, switch to another browser, use VPNs, or modify DNS settings. GoGuardian solves this with Chromebook-level integration that's not available on Windows/Mac. The founder needs deep systems programming expertise, not just web development skills.

### Prompt 4.2

> The founder mentions being "a software developer and early implementer of Agentic Coding." What type of software developer? The materials don't specify: web developer, mobile developer, systems programmer, data engineer? The technical demands of a kiosk-mode browser (OS-level process control, browser engine, content filtering, security hardening) require systems-level expertise. Does the founder demonstrate awareness of these technical challenges, or is the description abstract enough to suggest the technical complexity may be underestimated?

**Reasoning:** There's a meaningful difference between "I'm a React developer who can build web apps" and "I'm a systems programmer who can build OS-level kiosk mode software." The materials describe the founder as "a software developer" without specifics. The product is described at a feature level ("Study Mode button," "filtered search," "kiosk mode") rather than an architecture level (how it's implemented, what browser engine it uses, how it prevents bypass). This could mean the founder has deep expertise and is just summarizing for the course, OR it could mean the technical complexity hasn't been fully scoped. Agent 5 should flag this as an unknown.

## Section 5: Distribution Ability

### Prompt 5.1

> The founder's distribution plan is: (a) social media as the primary channel (Reddit, TikTok, Discord study communities), (b) educator/teacher networks as the B2B channel. Evaluate the founder's ability to execute on each. Does the founder have a social media presence or content creation track record? Has the founder demonstrated the ability to reach educators at scale? What evidence exists that these channels will work?

**Reasoning:** The Day 4 channel selection gives social media a score of 19/20 (the highest) and says "I can create posts in these channels, share demo videos of the product and interact with users." But there's no evidence the founder has done this before. Social media marketing for B2C products requires consistent content creation, community engagement, and often a personal brand. "I can create posts" is intention, not evidence. The educator network channel is warmer — "I have contact with teacher/educator networks" — but the founder hasn't demonstrated the ability to convert network access into distribution. Agent 5 needs to assess whether the founder has distribution skills or is assuming they'll figure it out.

### Prompt 5.2

> The Day 4 campaign plan says: "I can start immediately, by getting to know the specific Social Media channel: posting questions in the community, interacting with other users etc." This is a learning plan, not a distribution plan. It means the founder hasn't yet built a presence in these communities. Assess the time and effort required to build enough credibility in Reddit, TikTok, and Discord study communities to drive 100+ waitlist signups in two weeks. Is this realistic for a first-time social media marketer working part-time?

**Reasoning:** Reddit, TikTok, and Discord communities are notoriously hostile to marketing. Reddit communities will downvote obvious self-promotion. TikTok requires video creation skills and algorithmic understanding. Discord study communities expect genuine participation before any self-promotion. The founder's plan to "start by getting to know the channel" is honest but reveals they're starting from zero. Building enough trust to promote a product typically takes weeks to months of genuine community participation — not days. The 2-week waitlist target of 100+ users may be unrealistic through organic social media alone, especially for someone who hasn't built a following.

### Prompt 5.3

> The founder has identified three potential investors: Brighteye Ventures, Emerge Education, and Sparkmind.vc. For each, the outreach strategy involves attending events, connecting on LinkedIn, or building relationships with portfolio founders. Assess the founder's fundraising distribution ability. Do they have direct access to any of these investors, or is every path a cold or semi-warm approach? How long does it typically take to go from "connect on LinkedIn" to a funded round for a pre-seed EdTech startup?

**Reasoning:** The Day 5 materials show thoughtful investor research — the founder has identified relevant, stage-appropriate, sector-specific investors. But the outreach strategies are all relationship-building plays that take 3-6+ months. "Attend their annual Brighteye Live event in London" is a single annual touchpoint. "Connect with a venture partner on LinkedIn" is cold outreach with a <5% response rate. The founder explicitly says "NOT READY FOR INVESTORS" — which is self-aware but also means fundraising is months away. Agent 5 should assess whether the founder has the patience, persistence, and network leverage to close a pre-seed round given these starting positions.

## Section 6: Clarity of Ambition

### Prompt 6.1

> Based on the practice materials, what is the founder's ambition level for this company? Is this intended to be (a) a venture-scale company (aiming for $100M+ in revenue, planning to raise multiple rounds, building a large team), (b) a sustainable growth business (profitable at small scale, self-funded or bootstrapped, lifestyle-compatible), or (c) unclear? What evidence in the materials points toward each interpretation?

**Reasoning:** The One-Pager mentions "100 million devices" (suggesting large ambition) but the execution roadmap targets 50 users by June (suggesting cautious small-scale testing). The SWOT says "1-man team with a full-time job" (lifestyle constraint) but the investor research targets VC firms (venture ambition). The Day 5 materials say "NOT READY FOR INVESTORS" three times — which could mean "I want to raise but need traction first" or "I'm not sure I want to raise at all." This ambiguity matters because it affects every decision: VC-backed companies need to grow fast and capture market share; bootstrapped companies can grow slowly and focus on profitability. The founder seems to be straddling both paths.

### Prompt 6.2

> The founder describes the mission as dealing with "a real problem, not just for individual users but in overall society." Does the practice material show a founder driven by mission (solving the digital wellness crisis for children) or by opportunity (there's a market gap and they can build it)? Both are valid, but they lead to different companies. A mission-driven founder will persist through hardship because they care about the outcome. An opportunity-driven founder may pivot or abandon the idea if traction is slow. Which pattern does the evidence suggest?

**Reasoning:** The Day 3 focus sentence says the product "helps students to achieve sustained focus on their studies" — mission language. The SWOT Strengths say "the value proposition is clear, the direct selling-point is not AI, but to deal with a real problem" — also mission language. But the Day 4 channel selection is highly pragmatic ("Does it fit your product? Are your users already there? How fast will you get feedback?") — opportunity language. The healthiest founder profile combines both: mission provides persistence, opportunity-awareness provides pragmatism. Agent 5 should assess the balance.

### Prompt 6.3

> The founder writes "NOT READY FOR INVESTORS" three times in Day 5, and the investor table says "I still need to collect some real user feedback for my product, and metrics that show some kind of traction." Is this healthy self-awareness (knowing you're too early to raise) or a form of avoidance (using "not ready" as a reason to delay hard conversations)? What would "ready" look like for this founder, and is the current execution plan getting there fast enough?

**Reasoning:** Saying "not ready" is self-aware and shows the founder understands that pre-seed investors need at least minimal traction evidence. But it also means the founder is currently operating without investor pressure, timeline, or accountability — which for a solo part-time founder can lead to indefinite slow-building. The execution roadmap reaches 50 users by June 2026 with qualitative success metrics. Is 50 users with qualitative feedback enough to be "ready"? Probably not — most pre-seed EdTech investors want to see 200+ users, some revenue signal, and at least one institutional pilot. Agent 5 should assess whether the founder's definition of "ready" matches what investors actually require, and whether the timeline gets there.

## Section 7: Missing Capabilities and Risk Assessment

### Prompt 7.1

> The founder identifies needing "another software developer and a designer — who both care about the mission" and notes having "these as collaborators, but I need them full-time." Assess the team gap. Beyond a developer and designer, what other capabilities are missing for a K-12 EdTech startup? Consider: someone who understands school sales/procurement, someone with COPPA/FERPA compliance expertise, someone who can create social media content, someone with EdTech investor relationships. Rank the missing capabilities by urgency and impact.

**Reasoning:** The SWOT Weakness mentions the developer and designer need explicitly. But the materials don't mention sales, compliance, or marketing hires — which are arguably more critical at this stage. The product can be built by a solo developer using agentic coding. The harder challenges are regulatory compliance (COPPA deadline April 2026), school sales (if pursuing B2B), and content creation (for the social media channel). A common technical founder blind spot is assuming the hard part is building the product, when the hard part is actually selling it and keeping it legal.

### Prompt 7.2

> Write a Founder Fit Scorecard that rates the founder across six dimensions: (a) founder-market fit (1-10), (b) domain expertise (1-10), (c) execution ability (1-10), (d) technical depth (1-10), (e) distribution ability (1-10), (f) clarity of ambition (1-10). For each dimension, provide the score, evidence from the materials, and the single biggest risk. Then provide an overall Founder Score with a brief narrative assessment.

**Reasoning:** This is Agent 5's primary deliverable — a structured, evidence-based evaluation of the founder. The six dimensions map directly to the agent's role description. The scoring should be honest and grounded in what the materials actually show, not what could be inferred charitably. The narrative should be constructive — not "this founder can't do it" but "here's what needs to change for this founder to succeed."
