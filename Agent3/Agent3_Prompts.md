# Agent 3 — Market & Competition Analyst: Prompts

## 1. Market Category

**1.1** The product is a "kiosk-mode learning environment" browser that blocks distractions for K-12 students. What is the most accurate market category? Consider EdTech, parental controls / digital wellness, student productivity tools, and enterprise device management (MDM/UEM). Identify the primary category and adjacent categories.

**1.2** The founder claims existing tools are "either insufficient or block too much." Does this middle-ground positioning represent a genuine gap, or do existing players already address it? Search for products combining content filtering with a focused browsing/learning environment for students.

## 2. TAM / SAM / SOM

**2.1** Starting from "roughly 100 million devices used by K-12 students in Europe + USA," estimate TAM, SAM, and SOM for a subscription-based focus/kiosk browser. Assume B2C (parents/teachers) and B2B (school-wide licenses) as revenue streams. Use current EdTech and digital wellness spending data for K-12.

**2.2** The founder plans B2C first, then B2B, but notes "education institutions are tight on budget." What is realistic willingness-to-pay for each segment? Find comparable pricing for similar K-12 EdTech or parental control tools and estimate a price range for both B2C and B2B.

## 3. Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

**3.1** Is the market for student-focused browsing/digital wellness tools red ocean or blue ocean? Map players across the parental control space (Bark, Qustodio, Net Nanny, Securly, GoGuardian) and the student focus space (Forest, Freedom, Cold Turkey). Plot which compete on blocking vs. enabling focused work, and where a kiosk-mode browser sits.

**3.2** The founder claims "a significantly different design." Evaluate whether this is genuine blue ocean value innovation (creating new demand) or a feature improvement within an existing red ocean. What would need to be true for this to be a blue ocean play?

## 4. Direct and Indirect Competitors

**4.1** Identify direct competitors to a kiosk-mode focus browser for K-12 students. For each: product name, what it does, target user (parent/school/student), pricing model, and approximate scale (users, revenue, or funding). Focus on products combining content filtering with a structured browsing or learning environment.

**4.2** Identify indirect competitors — products solving the same problem (student distraction) differently: general focus apps, school device management platforms (GoGuardian, Securly, Jamf School), browser extensions, and dedicated learning tablets. For each, note how a buyer might choose it instead of a kiosk-mode browser.

**4.3** Create a competition map plotting competitors on two axes: (1) degree of restriction (light nudge → full lockdown) and (2) target buyer (consumer/parent → institutional/school). Where does the product sit, and is there an underserved quadrant?

## 5. AI / Big Tech Risk

**5.1** Assess the risk that Google, Apple, and Microsoft build native focus/kiosk features into their OS or browsers (Family Link, Screen Time, Family Safety, ChromeOS school management). How close are these built-in tools to replicating the product's value proposition? Rate the platform risk severity.

**5.2** Could AI-native startups leapfrog this product? If AI tutors (Khanmigo, GPT-based study assistants) become the standard study interface, does the "blocking" approach become obsolete? The founder sees AI cheating as an opportunity — could AI tutors flip it into a threat?

## 6. Crowdedness and Attractiveness Score

**6.1** Rate market crowdedness (1-10). Consider: number of funded competitors, platform-native solutions, ease of switching, and whether new entrants are still emerging. Justify the score.

**6.2** Compile a Market Attractiveness Score (1-10) weighing: (a) TAM size and growth, (b) competitive density, (c) Big Tech platform risk, (d) buyer willingness to pay, (e) regulatory tailwinds/headwinds (COPPA, GDPR-K), and (f) timing — the "brain rot" and screen time cultural moment. Explain each factor's contribution.

## 7. Market Note

**7.1** Write a concise market note (300-500 words) summarizing: market category, TAM/SAM/SOM, red vs. blue ocean assessment, top 3-5 competitors and positioning, AI/Big Tech risk level, and overall market attractiveness score. Frame as an internal analyst note for an investment committee evaluating this startup at pre-seed stage. Be candid about both opportunities and risks.
